Classifying Cars by Fuel Efficiency using mpg01

By: Your Name

Date: November 2025

Overview

In this project, I explored how different vehicle characteristics relate to fuel efficiency using the Auto.csv dataset. By converting MPG into a binary classification variable called mpg01, I was able to separate vehicles into low vs. high efficiency classes relative to the median MPG.

Loading the Data


import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

Autodata = pd.read_csv('Auto.csv')
df = pd.DataFrame(Autodata)

mpg_median = df['mpg'].median()
print(mpg_median, ": is the median of Miles Per Gallon")

df['mpg01'] = (df['mpg'] >= mpg_median).astype(int)
print(df)

df['horsepower'] = pd.to_numeric(df['horsepower'], errors='coerce')
df = df.dropna(subset=['horsepower'])

We found that the median MPG of the dataset is 23.0. Cars with MPG ≥ 23 are labeled as 1 and those below are 0 .

Visualization The Data Based on Instructions

The following code generates boxplots for several continuous features:


features = ['displacement', 'horsepower', 'weight', 'acceleration']
fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 2, figsize=(15, 12))
axes = axes.flatten()

for i, feature in enumerate(features):
    sns.boxplot(ax=axes[i], data=df, x='mpg01', y=feature)
    axes[i].set_title(f'Boxplot of {feature.capitalize()} by mpg01')
    axes[i].set_xlabel('mpg01 (0=Low, 1=High)')

And for categorical features:


features2 = ['origin', 'cylinders']
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(15, 6))
axes2 = axes.flatten()

for i, features2 in enumerate(features2):

Interpretation

Based on these visualizations, we can observe how different car attributes vary between fuel-efficient and fuel-inefficient vehicles. The most useful features for distinguishing between high and low MPG vehicles appear to be:

  • Weight – heavier cars trend toward lower MPG
  • Displacement – larger engines correlate with lower fuel efficiency
  • Horsepower – more powerful engines tend to consume more fuel
  • Cylinders – cars with more cylinders generally have lower MPG
  • Acceleration – shows some difference, but less pronounced
  • Year – newer models tend to have higher MPG

Based on these 6 graphs that we have, 6 categories: displacement, horsepower, weight, acceleration, year, cylinders. When interpretting these graphs, the key function to look at are the overlaps and specifically the location of them. If the 2 boxes are aligned with eachother, or at least in similar locations, it means that there isn’t much difference in the 2 categories. This means that the average, or the line in the middle of the boxes is around the same even if they are above or below the median for MPG. Based on this, we can determine that the categories that are the most comparitive with statistics like this are the weight, displacement, horsepower, cylinders, year, and acceleration, from most to least useful. We can see that the weight, displacement, and horsepower are the most useful in determining this, due to them being directly related to how much the car has to move, and this determined how much fuel is used up because of it.You want the ones below the median to be much more near the bottom of the graph, which shows us that it uses less gas per mile, which is what we want.

In short: cars that are lighter, newer, and have smaller engines tend to be more fuel efficient — which makes intuitive sense.
